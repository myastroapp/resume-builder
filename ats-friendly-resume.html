How to make an ATS-friendly resume

Most mid-size and large employers run resumes through an applicant tracking system (ATS) before a human reads them. If the software can't parse your resume or doesn't see the right keywords, a qualified application can be filtered out. Here's how to make sure yours gets through — then a free tool to check it.

How an ATS reads your resume

The system parses your file into structured fields — contact, work history, education, skills — then scores it against the job description, largely by matching keywords. Two things get you filtered out: a layout it can't parse, and missing the terms the role asks for.

Formatting: keep it parseable

Do: a single-column layout; standard section headings (Experience, Education, Skills); a common font; real bullet points; dates as MM/YYYY; save as .docx or a simple text-based PDF.
Don't: put key info in tables, columns, text boxes, headers/footers, or images; use exotic fonts or graphics for content; rely on icons to convey meaning. Many parsers drop or scramble these.

Keywords: mirror the posting

  • Use the exact terms from the job description — write "JavaScript," not "JS," if that's what the posting says. Matching is literal.
  • Put core skills in a dedicated Skills section and show them in your experience bullets.
  • Spell out then abbreviate once: "Search Engine Optimization (SEO)" catches both forms.
  • Never invent skills or keyword-stuff — once you clear the ATS, a human reads it, and an interview will expose padding.
