Which resume format should you use?

There are three standard resume formats. For the vast majority of people the answer is simple — reverse-chronological — but here's when each makes sense and why the others are riskier.

Build a reverse-chronological resume →

1. Reverse-chronological (recommended for most)

Lists your work history newest-first, with a skills section. It's what recruiters and applicant tracking systems expect, so it parses cleanly and reads fast.

Use it if: you have a steady work history in (or moving toward) the same field — i.e. almost everyone. This is the safe default.

2. Functional (skills-based)

Groups your experience by skill rather than by job, downplaying dates. The problem: recruiters distrust it (it's associated with hiding gaps), and ATS parsers often mis-read it.

Use it rarely: only if dates would genuinely mislead — and even then, a combination format is usually better.

3. Combination (hybrid)

A short skills/summary section up top, followed by a normal reverse-chronological history. Good middle ground for career changers or people with very relevant skills to surface early.

Use it if: you're changing careers or have standout skills you want a recruiter to see in the first five seconds — while still showing a clear timeline.

The bottom line

  • Default to reverse-chronological — it's expected, ATS-safe, and easiest to skim.
  • Use combination if you're pivoting careers.
  • Avoid pure functional unless you have a specific reason — it raises flags.
  • Whatever you pick: single column, standard headings, real text (see ATS tips).
